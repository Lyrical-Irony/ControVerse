About: Why Irony?

We chose irony as the focus of our project because it is a complex, elusive, and deeply relational concept. What intrigued us most is its inherent duality—the constant coexistence and tension between two layers of meaning: appearance and reality, the literal and the implied. This dynamic interplay between what is said and what is meant seemed particularly compelling to explore through an ontological approach.

An ontology allows us to make this dual structure visible: to represent the relationships, oppositions, and hidden connections that define irony as both a linguistic and cognitive phenomenon. In this way, the ontology becomes a tool for mapping how irony operates across different contexts, revealing the subtle balance between expression and interpretation.

We also considered irony as a form of creativity, in which both the ironist and the observer participate as co-creators. Irony, in this sense, is not a solitary act but a shared artistic process: its meaning emerges only through the interplay between the one who produces it and the one who perceives it. The ironist shapes the ambiguity, while the observer completes the act by recognizing and interpreting it. This collaborative creation is precisely what makes irony fascinating to represent within an ontology—an attempt to map not only the structure of meanings, but also the dynamic relationship that brings them to life.

Muecke's POV

Irony is notoriously difficult to define, as it is marked by a strong degree of subjectivity. Attempts to confine such a broad concept within a structured framework have been numerous, yet inevitably reductive. The challenge is particularly acute in literature, where multiple forms of irony have already been contextualized and analyzed by scholars in relation to different authors.

A further complication arises from the persistent tendency to classify irony as either “good” or “bad.” This has produced a terminological disorder and fostered the use of partial definitions or, worse, qualitative distortions. The insistence on a binary distinction between “effective” and “ineffective” irony has, in fact, impeded the construction of a neutral and taxonomic conceptual apparatus.

Therefore, for Muecke, the figure of the ironologist appears necessary. Their role is not to interpret, but rather to simplify and generalize the phenomenon.

At its core, irony may be described as the “art of saying something without truly saying it.” It constitutes an intellectual instrument, associated with prose and rational discourse rather than with lyrical emotion. Muecke reconstructs its historical development, emphasizing the decisive expansion initiated by German Romanticism at the end of the eighteenth century.

In this period, the concept was broadened from a mere rhetorical trope to a generalized philosophical stance, transforming irony into an essential tool for confronting the contradictions and aporias inherent in the human condition.

"irony, like beauty, is in the eye of the beholder and is not a quality inherent in any remark, event, or situation." [Muecke]

The key element for irony to take shape is therefore an observer with a sense of irony. The ironologist's goal is not to define irony itself, but to "define the formal requirements of an ironical remark or an ironic situation."

Irony is based on the "double exposure" (duality) of two levels of meaning or reality that collide:

  1. Level 1 (Appearance): The immediately perceived reality, the literal meaning, the stated intention, or a limited understanding of a situation.
  2. Level 2 (Reality): The true meaning, the hidden intention, the superior understanding of the situation (typically shared by the ironist and the observer).

The presence of both levels is crucial. Irony does not merely correct a falsehood but exposes two realities simultaneously.
